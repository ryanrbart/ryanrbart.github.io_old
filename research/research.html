Current Projects

Vegetation Change and Streamflow in the Sierra Nevada

I am currently investigating how streamflow in California's Sierra Nevada may respond to vegetation change induced by climate change and/or disturbance. The goal of this project is to integrate field measurements of water use among different types of vegetation into the Regional Hydro-Ecologic Simulation System (Rhessys) model in order to assess the potential impact on streamflow under a range of climate scenarios. The research watersheds are located at the Southern Sierra Critical Zone Observatory (CZO) and this project represents a collaborative effort with Max Moritz (UC Berkeley) and Naomi Tague (UC Santa Barbara) with funding provided by the California Energy Commission.

 

 

Past Projects

Dissertation

Wildfire is a major source of episodic land-cover change in California watersheds, altering numerous hydrologic processes and impacting both water resources planning and ecosystem management. For my dissertation, I investigated the regional effect of wildfire on streamflow in California watersheds. A mixed-modeling approach was used to regionalize both annual streamflow and baseflow recession rates response to wildfire. In addition, the effect of antecedent storage on inter-seasonal baseflow recession rate variability was also examined.

 

BAR (Basin Assessment for Regionalization) Project

I worked with Allen Hope on the NASA Regional Hydrological Response of Semi-Arid Mediterranean Climate Watersheds to Land-Cover/Land-Use Variability project. We incorporated remote sensing (MODIS) data to improve predictions of streamflow in ungauged basins and evaluated streamflow response to fire in southern California and invasive vegetation in the Western Cape Region of South Africa. The research had an explicit focus on modeling uncertainty and we investigated storage configurations and parameter stability of the IHACRES rainfall-runoff model.
